Definitions

100-year flood

A flood event that has a 1 percent chance of being equaled or exceeded in any given year. (This term does not mean a flood that happens once a century. And it does not mean a flood that has a 1 percent chance of happening every year.)

25th Street Viaduct

A 1.5-mile-long elevated freight track running through South Philadelphia, dividing the neighborhoods Grays Ferry (on its west) from Point Breeze and West Passyunk (on its east). It was finished in 1928 by the Pennsylvania Railroad, complete with loading bays, in anticipation of a new corridor of factories. Instead came the Great Depression, WWII, and postwar deindustrialization. By the late twentieth century, the viaduct had become an unsightly and dangerous relic that regularly shed debris onto the street below. Auto repair shops became the main businesses in the shadow of squat Ionic columns made of steel-reinforced concrete. Now owned by CSX, the 25th Street Viaduct is still in use. In the 2010s, trains carrying crude oil from North Dakota’s Bakken formation regularly rolled through Philadelphia over this line.

Act 2

A short name for a package of laws passed by the Pennsylvania General Assembly in 1995 to encourage voluntary cleanup and reuse of brownfields and other contaminated commercial and industrial sites. Also known as the “Land Recycling Program,” Act 2 established standards for environmental review and remediation, and processes for liability relief and financial assistance. The program is administered by the DEP.

aerial views

In context of this website, these are oblique-angle (“bird’s-eye”) photographs taken from airplanes.

Area(s) of Interest (AOI)

In 2003, Sunoco entered into a new consent order and agreement with the DEP to investigate and remediate legacy pollution at its South Philadelphia refinery complex. For administrative purposes, Sunoco divided the complex into eleven Areas of Interest (AOI), ten aboveground, one underground. Responsibility for pre-2012 pollution, including subsurface contamination, still belongs to Sunoco (and its parent company, Energy Transfer), even though the refinery complex has since changed ownership, twice, and the facility has been demolished. The northern portion of AOI-1, the Belmont Terminal, is now treated as a separate remediation unit. Areas of Interest 1–4 comprise the “south yard” of the historic Atlantic/ARCO refinery at Point Breeze, with AOI-8 being the “north yard” and AOI-10 being the “west yard.” Areas 5, 6, 7, and 9 comprise the historic Gulf (later Chevron) refinery at Girard Point. AOI-9 is also known as the Schuylkill River Tank Farm. A Sunoco subsidiary, Evergreen Resources Group, LLC, has managed cleanup of the AOIs since 2013. Evergreen and its consultants have generated significant public documentation about each site within the former refinery complex. Most of these environmental remediation reports are browsable and downloadable on this website.

Atlantic refinery

A corporate place name that can refer to any of the following:
  1. A foundational facility under the ownership of Atlantic Petroleum Storage Company (Warden, Frew & Co.) that began distilling petroleum in 1870 at Point Breeze on the Schuylkill River, immediately south of the Passyunk Avenue gas works. The location is today’s Area of Interest 2.
  2. A refinery in two parcels (the original “south yard,” or Areas of Interest 1–4, and the subsequent “north yard,” or Area of Interest 8) operated by Atlantic Refining under the control of the Standard Oil trust (personified by John D. Rockefeller) from 1874–1911. The “north yard” was also called the “Philadelphia yard” because it originated as a separate business concern of the Philadelphia Refining Company before being purchased by Rockefeller.
  3. That two-parcel refinery as owned by Atlantic Refining—and its corporate successor, Atlantic-Richfield (ARCO)—as a newly independent corporation after the federal dissolution of the Standard Oil trust in 1911. Atlantic/ARCO operated the refinery at Point Breeze until 1985.
  4. That legacy refinery in the period 1985–1988, when it was owned by Dutch oil trader John Deuss through a company called Atlantic Petroleum Corporation.
  5. That legacy refinery in the period 1988–1994, when Sun Company (later Sunoco) owned and operated it, but before Sun purchased the neighboring refinery at Girard Point and integrated their operations to form one South Philadelphia refinery complex.

Bartram’s Garden

A public park near Kingsessing on the site where John Bartram (1699–1777), prominent Quaker and co-founder of the American Philosophical Society, established the first botanic garden in what would become the United States. Railroad engineer Andrew McCalla Eastwick (1811–1879) purchased Bartram’s plant collection for his country estate. The City of Philadelphia assumed ownership of the property in 1891. By that point, the green space was an orphan, surrounded on all sides by polluting industries and polluted sites. Bartram’s Garden was designated a National Historic Landmark in 1960. Today (2025), a new swing bridge on the Schuylkill Banks trail promises to better connect the park (and Southwest Philadelphia more generally) to Center City.

Bartram’s North

A complex of brownfields between Grays Avenue and the Schuylkill River between 49th Street and 51st Street, just upstream from Bartram’s Garden. As of 2025, some of the “Bartram’s North” property is owned by the Philadelphia Authority for Industrial Development (PAID). The land here has been eyed for redevelopment since at least 2013, when the City of Philadelphia published the Lower Schuylkill Master Plan. Bartram’s North still has legacy pollution from a number of former on-site industries: oil storage, paint production, garbage reduction, and waste incineration. The site also attracted illegal dumping in its period of post-industrial abandonment. The so-called “51st Street Terminal” was operated between 1951 and 2021 by various petroleum companies, including Allied Oil, Hess Oil, Plains Products Terminals, and PBF Logistics. Separately, Getty Oil had a tank farm near the “49th Street Terminal.” The brownfields here are not to be confused with “Bartram’s South” at Gibson Point, though the Bartram’s Mile section of the Schuylkill Banks trail passes by both. A leak of chromium, a toxic substance, was discovered on the trail in July 2024, which led to investigation and remediation plans for the 51st Street Terminal.

Belmont Terminal

The Belmont Terminal, located at Passyunk Avenue and 28th Street, is a former distribution facility within the South Philadelphia refinery complex where fuel delivery trucks loaded gasoline and diesel to supply service stations in Greater Philadelphia. The property was not included in HRP’s 2020 purchase of the South Philadelphia refinery complex. It is still owned by Sunoco (though its parent company, Energy Transfer). Formerly designated within Area of Interest 1, the Belmont Terminal is now treated as a separate (non-numbered) remediation area.

Bellwether District

The commercial name of the ongoing redevelopment project (2020–) at the former South Philadelphia refinery complex, as branded by the current owner, HRP. According to HRP’s promotional material, the property is being designed and built for e-commerce, logistics, and life sciences. The surface area of the Bellwether District is nearly equivalent to that of the Center City core commercial district.

brownfield(s)

Former industrial or commercial site(s) where future use is affected by real and/or perceived environmental contamination. In 1988, the United States Conference of Mayors began publishing an annual report, Recycling America’s Land: A National Report on Brownfields Redevelopment. In 1993, the EPA began issuing grants to local governments to launch pilot projects for remediating brownfields. In 2002, the US Congress passed (and George W. Bush signed) the Small Business Liability Relief and Brownfields Revitalization Act, which codified the approach of incentivizing cleanup of urban brownfields rather than punishing past polluters. In the United States, local and/or state agencies oversee the remediation of brownfields, whereas federal agencies oversee the remediation of more severely contaminated sites on the Superfund National Priorities List. The Lowermost Schuylkill River petrochemical corridor contains many brownfields, but no Superfund sites.

Center City

A place name that can denote any of three things:
  1. The original area of Philadelphia, as laid out as a grid by founder William Penn in 1682, and redefined in the 1854 Act of Consolidation that created Philadelphia County.
  2. Contemporary downtown Philadelphia, including the main commercial and historical neighborhoods, which largely conforms to the original area of the city. The eastern and western boundaries—the Delaware and Schuylkill rivers—are obvious; the southern boundary is aptly named South Street. The current northern boundary, depending on one’s point of view, is Spring Garden Street or the sunken Vine Street Expressway.
  3. Center City District: the area zoned as “Center City Core Commercial Mixed-Use” and the non-profit organization that works with the Central Philadelphia Development Corporation to enhance the vitality of downtown.

Commonwealth

The Commonwealth of Pennsylvania, the official name of the Keystone State.

DEP

Pennsylvania Department of Environmental Protection, founded in 1995 (though its administrative precursor, the Department of Environmental Resources, dates to 1970).

DSCP

Defense Supply Center Philadelphia, historically known as Philadelphia Quartermaster Depot. This federal facility at 21st Street and Oregon Avenue opened in the 1920s and closed in the 1990s. It supplemented and ultimately replaced the Schuylkill Arsenal (founded in 1799) at 2620 Grays Ferry Road. Throughout its long history, the arsenal/depot made textiles for the military, including uniforms and flags. In 1987, state investigators verified the existence of a large plume of LNAPL, contamination from the Atlantic refinery, beneath the facility and neighboring Passyunk Homes. This website contains many documents related to the remediation of the site, which has since been decommissioned and commercially redeveloped as Quartermaster Plaza, a shopping center that includes a Home Depot. One of the parking lots in the plaza contains an active recovery well. (The functions of DSCP have been replaced by Defense Logistics Agency Philadelphia Troop Support, located in North Philadelphia.)

Eastwick

The southernmost residential part of Southwest Philadelphia, situated between Cobbs Creek, Darby Creek, and Tinicum Marsh on the west; Philadelphia International Airport, I-76, and a sewage treatment plant on the south; the Lowermost Schuylkill River petrochemical corridor on the east; and railroad tracks on all sides. Various names, including “The Meadows” and the “Far Southwest” have been applied to this low-lying section of the county. The name “Eastwick” (after a former owner of Bartram's Garden) dates to the 1920s, when developers and allied residents lobbied for improved city services through the Greater Eastwick Neighborhood Association. The name became fixed in the 1950s as part of the Eastwick Redevelopment Area Plan by the Philadelphia Planning Commission, which sought to “renew” this semi-marshy, semi-rural, semi-industrial and largely impoverished neighborhood. Flooding is a perennial problem in Eastwick—a problem expected to worsen with global warming. The example of Eastwick is widely cited in literature on environmental justice.

environmental justice (EJ)

A political movement and regulatory practice that emphasizes fair treatment and meaningful involvement of all people—regardless of race, ethnicity, national origin, area of residence, income, disability, educational level, etc.—in regard to the development, implementation, and enforcement of environmental law. The primary goal of EJ is to prevent any group of people from suffering disproportionate negative effects (including risks) relating to environmental health. In the United States, EJ grew out of the Black civil rights movement. In the 1980s, sociologist Robert D. Bullard published foundational research. Outside academia, a landmark 1987 report from the United Church of Christ (UCC), “Toxic Wastes and Race in the United States,” identified examples of “environmental racism.” The UCC’s Racial Justice Commission went on the sponsor the First National People of Color Environmental Leadership Summit in 1991. The decade of the 1990s was a high point for EJ, with President Bill Clinton writing its principles into federal administrative guidelines, and with activists in postcolonial settings around the world adapting and expanding the movement. In the US, however, EJ became increasingly partisan in the first quarter of the twenty-first century, with deep political disagreements over the meaning and proper focus of equity: A) a historically informed effort to repair past and ongoing environmental injustices to disadvantaged groups and vulnerable populations (“fenceline communities”), or B) a future-oriented effort to deliver environmental goods to all people? In 2025, the second administration of Donald J. Trump revoked executive orders, regulatory guidelines, grants, and research funding relating to racial equity and environmental justice, portraying those concepts as discriminatory.

EPA

US Environmental Protection Agency, founded in 1970 through an executive order by President Richard Nixon.

ESRI

Environmental Systems Research Institute, Inc., a California-based multinational geographic information system software company.

Evergreen

Evergreen Resources Group, LLC, a subsidiary of Sunoco (and its parent company, Energy Transfer) that manages legacy remediation at the Areas of Interest that comprise the former South Philadelphia refinery complex.

FEMA

Federal Emergency Management Agency, established by executive order in 1978, currently a division of the Department of Homeland Security.

fenceline communities

Populations—typically low income, and, in US context, disproportionately non-white—with inequitable exposures to environmental pollutants and/or inequitable risks of environmental harms due to residential proximity to one or more high-impact sites (e.g., oil refineries, military bases, landfills). This legalistic term, sometimes called “frontline communities,” is closely tied to the concept of environmental justice.

Gibson Point

A place name for the west bank of the Schuylkill River at the bend just downstream of Bartram's Garden. In the late nineteenth century, the US government paid for deep-water dredging from the mouth of the Schuylkill to “Gibson’s Point,” a distance of four miles. A collection of small, independent petroleum refineries arose here in the 1870s. In 1883, National Transit Company, a Standard Oil subsidiary, completed a 260-mile-long six-inch wrought-iron pipeline from an oil field near Colegrove, Pennsylvania, to Gibson Point, and thence under the river to the Atlantic refinery at Point Breeze. In 1888, Standard (John D. Rockefeller) acquired the oil works at Gibson Point, too. In 1929, United States Gypsum Corporation opened a manufacturing plant at Gibson Point, and in 1942 Socony-Vacuum added a tank farm at 58th Street and Schuylkill River. TransMontaigne later operated an oil terminal here. United States Gypsum closed in 1988, selling its property to a cement company. As of 2025, most of the post-petroleum brownfield at Gibson Point is owned by the Philadelphia Authority for Industrial Development (PAID), which calls the property “Bartram’s South.” Not to be confused with Bartram's North, though the Bartram’s Mile section of the Schuylkill Banks trail passes by both.

Girard Point

A place name with two meanings in Philadelphia:
  1. A topographic point at the mouth of the Schuylkill River where the backwater behind League Island (home of the Philadelphia Naval Yard) meets the channel. The I-95 bridge crosses the river at Girard Point.
  2. The real estate north of Girard Point, south of Yankee Point, and west of Lanier Avenue and the Pennsylvania Railroad tracks, where Gulf Oil Company built a refinery in the 1920s. The Girard Point refinery (later owned by Chevron, before being consolidated into the Sun-owned South Philadelphia refinery complex) comprises remediation Areas of Interest 5–7.

Grays Ferry

A place name with three meanings in Philadelphia:
  1. A crossing on the Schuylkill River, dating to at least the seventeenth century, which takes its name after George Gray, who owned the ferry in the Revolutionary era. The ferry was eventually obviated by a railroad bridge in 1838 and a streetcar bridge in 1901.
  2. A former industrial zone, dominated by the chemical industry (Henry Bower, Harrison Brothers, Kalion, Barrett, Allied, DuPont), that occupied most of the land on the south side of the Schuylkill River by the Grays Ferry crossing. In 2010, the University of Pennsylvania acquired a remediated brownfield here for its Pennovation Works. The section of the Schuylkill Banks trail on the northern perimeter of the site has been named Grays Ferry Crescent.
  3. A neighborhood of rowhouses and public housing projects located just east the former refinery complex and chemical works. Confusingly, the residential blocks closest to the old Grays Ferry crossing are effectively not part of the Grays Ferry neighborhood. Rather, the name Forgotten Bottom has been applied to this blocked-off micro-neighborhood south of Pennovation Works, north of the CSX railroad tracks, and east of the Schuylkill Expressway.

HOLC

Home Owners’ Loan Corporation, a government-sponsored corporation created by the US Congress in 1933 through the Home Owners’ Loan Corporation Act. The corporation had the mandate—part of the New Deal of President Franklin Delano Roosevelt—to expand home-buying opportunities and prevent home foreclosures through the (re)financing of mortgages. In effect, HOLC helped white Americans achieve and maintain homeownership more than non-white Americans. From 1935–1940, the HOLC compiled social data on residents in every major US city and used them to create mortgage security risk maps. On the HOLC’s color-coded maps, red represented “hazardous” risk to banks. Neighborhoods with Blacks (and, to varying extent, Jews and certain immigrants) automatically received this “D-grade” designation. The term “redlining” has since been applied to the prejudicial attitudes and segregated outcomes of federal housing policy.

HRP

HRP Group—formerly known as Hilco Redevelopment Partners—a Chicago-based real estate investment firm that specializes in large-scale redevelopment projects. In 2020, with the approval of a bankruptcy judge, HRP acquired the South Philadelphia refinery complex—about 1.5 percent the landmass of Philadelphia County—for $252 million. Having since razed the main refinery, HRP is now redeveloping the site into the 1,300-acre “Bellwether District,” designed for logistics and biotech. HRP’s project is the beneficiary of a Keystone Opportunity Zone.

just transition

A phrase that originated in the US labor movement of the 1980s, later adapted by climate activists globally. The “transition” refers to a hoped-for “energy transition” away from fossil fuels—i.e., decarbonization to the point of a “low-carbon economy.” The “just” refers to equity—i.e., an energy transition that is fair as possible to everyone concerned, including laborers and communities historically tied to the fossil fuel sector, so that no one is left behind.

Keystone Opportunity Zone (KOZ)

A program authorized by the Pennsylvania General Assembly in 1998 and administered through the commonwealth’s Department of Community and Economic Development, that provides business incentives (in the form of tax credits, tax waivers, or tax abatements) for the redevelopment of abandoned, unused, or underutilized land and buildings. Each KOZ has its own expiration date. In Philadelphia County, KOZs are managed through the city’s Department of Commerce. The in-progress Bellwether District by HRP has benefitted from this program.

Kingsessing

An urban neighborhood that adjoins Bartram's Garden and Gibson Point, and that includes the public housing project Bartram Village. Originally, this name (of Lenape derivation) denoted what is now called Southwest Philadelphia. The 1854 Act of Consolidation incorporated Kingsessing Township into Philadelphia County. In the nineteenth century, this township between Cobbs Creek and the Schuylkill River was dotted with hamlets, including Elmwood, Paschalville, and Bell Road.

LNAPL

Light nonaqueous phase liquids—a petrochemical pollutant. This kind of hydrocarbon is insoluble in water. It also has a lower density than water. Therefore, when it escapes into the ground, LNAPL stops at the depth of the water table. A vast plume of LNAPL formed under the Atlantic refinery at Point Breeze over the decades, and migrated eastward, as publicly verified in 1987. This plume negatively affected the DSCP and also Passyunk Homes. In South Philadelphia, recovery of this LNAPL plume was most intense in the 1990s, but remediation at Quartermaster Depot is ongoing.

low-angle views

In context of this website, these are visual images that take a perspective from river level, or street level, or bridge level.

Lowermost Schuylkill River

The final 8.5-mile stretch of the river below Fairmount Water Works, dividing South Philadelphia from Southwest Philadelphia. This is the tidal section of the river, and historically the most industrialized and polluted section. In particular, the 6.5-mile stretch from South Street Bridge to the Delaware River once featured a high concentration of polluting industries: coal-powered generating stations, chemical works, gas works, oil refineries, tank yards and fuel terminals, garbage reduction facilities, trash incinerators, slaughterhouses, railroads and railyards, automobile junkyards, a gypsum plant, a sewage treatment plant, and the Philadelphia Naval Yard. Large cargo ships could navigate upstream for four miles, stopping at Point Breeze or Gibson Point. The lowermost Schuylkill also includes two green spaces from Philadelphia’s preindustrial era: Bartram's Garden and The Woodlands (both National Historic Landmarks). The soon-to-be-completed Schuylkill Banks trail has added a recreational component to the former petrochemical corridor.

NGO

Non-governmental organization: an entity, generally not for profit, that operates independently from government (though sometimes with government funding) to address matters of social concern. In context of this website, Philadelphia-based NGOs include the Clean Air Council, the Pew Charitable Trusts, Philly Thrive, and the Schuylkill River Development Corporation. Documents and reports generated by these and other NGOs are browsable and downloadable through this website.

NOAA

National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration, reorganized in 1970 as scientific agency within the Department of Commerce (preceded by the US Coast and Geodetic Survey).

OSHA

Occupational Safety and Health Administration, a regulatory agency within the US Department of Labor, as established by Congress in 1970.

Passyunk Avenue gas works

The legacy PGW facility at 3100 Passyunk Avenue, near Point Breeze on the Schuylkill River, sandwiched between two parts of the South Philadelphia refinery complex (and now the Bellwether District).

Passyunk Homes

Originally a federal housing project for Navy personnel on 56 acres in deep South Philadelphia. It opened under management of Philadelphia Housing Authority (PHA) in 1941. In the postwar period, Passyunk Homes became a regular housing project, albeit a less desirable one given its remote and hemmed-in location between railroad tracks and highways, and its proximity to the oil refineries at Girard Point and Point Breeze. In the late 1980s, investigators verified the existence of a plume of LNAPL about 12 feet below Passyunk Homes. Soon thereafter, the US Department of Housing and Urban Development informed the PHA that it would not fund badly needed repairs at the contaminated site. Not until 1999 did the PHA condemn Passyunk Homes and issue evacuation orders. In 2006, after remediation, the City sold the residential brownfield to a private developer, which has since built a neighborhood called Siena Place.

Pennovation Works

A 23-acre technology park and innovation hub near Grays Ferry owned by the University of Pennsylvania at the remediated site of a former chemical works.

petrochemical

In context of this website, this term applies to any fossil fuel (coal, petroleum, natural gas) or derivative therefrom, including LNAPL, liquid fuels, chemicals, and plastics. Under distillation, petroleum separates into different fractions of hydrocarbons, each with a different boiling point. These range from light fractions such as propane, butane, gasoline, and naphtha; middle distillates such as kerosene, diesel fuel, jet fuel, and heating oil; and heavy fractions such as lubricating oil, heavy fuel oil, paraffin wax, and bitumen. At the molecular level, all fossil fuels share commonalities. For example, methane (a gas) can be derived from coal (a solid). That is why coal was the historic source for municipal gas for streetlamps in Philadelphia and many other cities. For a time, PGW enriched its manufactured coal-gas with naphtha, a petroleum distillate, from the Atlantic refinery. Coal, petroleum, and natural gas have very different histories of extraction and distribution. But from a certain perspective—notably the planetary perspective of climate change—distinctions between fossil fuels break down.

PGW

Philadelphia Gas Works, a City-owned and City-operated utility. Founded in 1836, PGW was for much of its history—from 1897 to 1972—leased to United Gas Improvement Company (UGI). The original purpose of the utility was to create gas from coal to illuminate streetlamps. Using coal-gas as a consumer fuel for residential heating and cooking did not become common until the early twentieth century. After WWII, by which point gaslights were being phased out, PGW transitioned to natural gas. Today, Philadelphia is the largest city in the United States that controls a fossil fuel utility.

Point Breeze

A place name with two meanings in Philadelphia:
  1. A topographical point at a bend in the Schuylkill River, historically the furthest upstream landing point for large ships, which made it an obvious location for a PGW facility and subsequently the Atlantic refinery. Two of Philadelphia’s prominent diagonal streets—Passyunk Avenue and Point Breeze Avenue—began as paths from Center City to the Schuylkill at Point Breeze.
  2. A twentieth-century rowhouse neighborhood located east of the 25th Street Viaduct (an elevated freight railroad) and south of Washington Avenue. Confusingly, the residential Point Breeze is not adjacent to the topographical Point Breeze. Rather, the Grays Ferry neighborhood is the closest residential area to the bend in the river.

Quartermaster Depot

In context of this website, the former location of the Defense Supply Center Philadelphia (DSCP) at 21st Street and Oregon Avenue, and the current location of Quartermaster Plaza, a shopping center. A large plume of LNAPL—contamination from the Atlantic refinery—contaminated the soil beneath the depot and neighboring Passyunk Homes, as publicly disclosed in 1987.

redlining

The practice of categorically denying access to mortgages to whole neighborhoods, not just individuals. From 1935–1940, the HOLC compiled social data on residents in every major US city and used them to create mortgage security risk maps. On the HOLC’s color-coded maps, red represented “hazardous” risk to banks. Neighborhoods with Blacks (and, to varying extent, Jews and certain immigrants) automatically received this “D-grade” designation.

Schuylkill Banks

An in-progress recreational trail along the length of the Lowermost Schuylkill River, from Fairmount Water Works to Pennovation Works to Bartram's Garden to Eastwick. The trail—which has necessitated the construction of boardwalks and bridges—is the project of the Schuylkill River Development Corporation (formerly Schuylkill River Development Council), a non-profit NGO founded in 1992.

Schuylkill Generating Station

A co-generation plant (producing electricity and steam heat) at 2600 Christian Street, just downstream from the South Street Bridge. The original building dates to 1903—Philadelphia Electric Company’s first large-scale, centralized coal-fired power plant in the Philadelphia region. In 1950, PECO connected the station to its district heating system (aka Philadelphia’s “steam loop”). The station eventually transitioned from coal to fuel oil, and then from fuel oil to natural gas. Its current owner, Vicinity Energy—which still supplies steam heat to the University of Pennsylvania and many corporate buildings in Center City—has announced long-term plans to decarbonize the plant.

Schuylkill River Tank Farm

Also known as Area of Interest 9, this shuttered storage and shipment facility on the west bank of the river in Southwest Philadelphia is part of the former South Philadelphia refinery complex. Originally operated by Gulf Oil, the 211-acre site is now owned by HRP, though it is not part of HRP’s initial Bellwether District. A Keystone Opportunity Zone was extended to the tank farm in 2024—tax relief to offset anticipated costs of demolition—and represents a future phase of HRP’s redevelopment project.

South Philadelphia

A large and socially varied district of Philadelphia County, formerly called Passyunk Township, that developed in the second half of the nineteenth century. Historically, South Philadelphia included everything south of South Street, north of Oregon Avenue, east of the Schuylkill River, and west of the Delaware River. Currently, as a residential zone, the boundaries are generally considered to be Washington Avenue on the north, I-95 on the east, and I-76 on the west and south. In the nineteenth century, much of this low-lying area—with truck farms and extensive marshes—was known as “the Neck.” After the Philadelphia Naval Shipyard moved to League Island in 1871, developers progressively filled in the marshes of the Neck, forming land that now hosts FDR Park and the South Philadelphia Sports Complex (and, before that, garbage dumps). The information on this website only relates to the Schuylkill side of South Philadelphia, east of South Broad Street.

South Philadelphia refinery complex

A petroleum facility on the Lowermost Schuylkill River that operated continuously, albeit in many different industrial and corporate forms, from 1866 to June 21, 2019, when a near-catastrophic explosion occurred. The complex included refining facilities, tank farms, and terminals for ships, trains, and trucks. It grew in fits and starts, with many conflagrations and innovations in the early decades. Many business interests invested in the complex, including Atlantic Petroleum Storage Company (Warden, Frew & Co.), Standard Oil (John D. Rockefeller), Atlantic Refining, Atlantic-Richfield (ARCO), Atlantic Petroleum Corporation (John Deuss), Gulf Oil Company, Chevron, Sun Company (later Sunoco), the Carlyle Group, and Philadelphia Energy Solutions (PES). For most of the twentieth century, the complex consisted of two separate entities: the original Atlantic refinery at Point Breeze (sandwiching the preexisting PGW plant) owned by Atlantic/ARCO, and the subsequent refinery at Girard Point owned by Gulf/Chevron. In 1994, Sun became the first corporation to own and operate the whole complex as an integrated operation. PES became the second consolidated owner in 2012. HRP became the third in 2020 and proceeded to raze and cap the refinery complex. HRP is not responsible for pre-2012 pollution and its ongoing remediation; that belongs to Sunoco through Evergreen, a subsidiary. Evergreen’s Areas of Interest effectively comprise the legal boundaries of the former South Philadelphia refinery complex.

Southwest Philadelphia

The lowest-elevation district of Philadelphia—some of it below sea level—which transitioned from horticultural to industrial in the early twentieth century, while remaining quite disconnected from Center City. It became part of Philadelphia County through the 1854 Act of Consolidation, which incorporated Kingsessing Township as the Twenty-seventh Ward. Today, the Southwest comprises the Fortieth Ward. The Southwest includes the neighborhoods Kingsessing, Elmwood, and Eastwick. The Southwest has relatively low population density because so much land here is zoned for non-residential uses, including the Philadelphia International Airport, the John Heinz National Wildlife Refuge at Tinicum, the Southwest Water Pollution Control Plant, the Schuylkill River Tank Farm, the Philadelphia Wholesale Produce Market, Mount Moriah Cemetery, Bartram’s Garden, and various earth material yards and automobile junkyards.

sustainability

In context of this website, this word means either of two things:
  1. Corporate branding related to the idea of “sustainable development”—that is, the assertion that capitalistic growth can meet present-day societal needs and preferences without jeopardizing the biosphere and future generations who will rely on it. For example, HRP presents its redevelopment plans for the Bellwether District as a “global model of sustainable development and design.”
  2. Corporate or municipal protocols for promoting quality of life while reducing greenhouse gas emissions. In the early twenty-first century, it became standard institutional practice for an “office of sustainability” to create a “climate action plan” for meeting the parent institution’s decarbonization goal. The City of Philadelphia and the University of Pennsylvania (Penn) separately released their first climate action plans in 2009. The City is unlikely to meet its goal (“carbon neutrality” or “net zero” by 2050) without decarbonizing the operations of PGW. Many reports generated Philadelphia’s Office of Sustainability and by Penn Sustainability are browsable and downloadable through this website.

Tasker Homes

Also called Tasker Street Housing Project. One of the earliest (1937) high-density projects by the Philadelphia Housing Authority, it occupied 40 acres near 30th Street and Tasker Street in Grays Ferry, close to the north yard of the Atlantic refinery. On at least two occasions—May 1970 and August 1983—residents of Tasker Homes had to be evacuated due to refinery accidents. In 2004, Tasker Homes was demolished to make way for low-density public housing, renamed Grays Ferry Estates.
